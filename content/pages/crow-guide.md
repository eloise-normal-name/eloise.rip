Title: Crow Guide
Slug: crow-guide
Summary: Everything I've learned from years of feeding and befriending crows — how to get started, what to feed them, common myths, and what to do if you find a baby.

This is a guide I've been meaning to write for a while. I've been feeding crows regularly for a few years now and have gotten to know quite a few of them personally. This is all stuff I've picked up through experience — things I wish I had known at the start.

---

## The Roster

These are the crows I've gotten to know by name. Identifying individual crows takes time — you mostly go by size, beak shape, behavior quirks, and which other crows they hang around with. Names come after.

### Merp & Berp

Merp is one of the first crows I really got to know individually. Their signature move is the "merp-dump" — grabbing a treat bag by the bottom and shaking everything out of it — which is partly how I recognize them. Very clever, very motivated, will actively seek me out rather than waiting to be found. Berp is usually nearby to pick up whatever Merp drops or misses. A good team.

### Groof & Groofriend

Groof is the larger one with a noticeably rounder beak. Bold, instigating, a little chaotic — Groof will grab a bag out of Groofriend's beak and has been known to set up a toll situation where both crows land directly in my path so I can't leave without giving them more treats. The "cute groof look" is a specific expression Groof does when asking for food that is extremely effective. Groofriend is slightly smaller and a bit more cautious but will absolutely snip back at Groof when pushed.

### Longlegs & Fussy

A mated pair with very different personalities. Longlegs is a dedicated stasher — they'll take treat after treat and cache them all, and have a foot issue that makes grabbing whole eggs tricky (I've learned to smoosh the egg a little so they can carry it). Fussy is picky about what they'll eat and makes spectacular messes doing it — just absolute chaos with an egg — but only their favorite things. Longlegs seems happy to receive an indefinite number of treats; Fussy has opinions.

### Boogie & Woogie

The balcony crows, named by Catgirl. They're very silly together. Mostly I know them from balcony visits and doing goofy things with nuts. Good vibes only from these two.

### Green Bandit

A crow with a green research band from UW, so presumably part of a study. I see them maybe once a month. Green Bandit is unusually relaxed and friendly, which might be a product of being around researchers a lot. Can carry two peanuts but not three, and will call a friend over to help with the third one rather than just leaving it. Their friend showed up once and immediately heisted an egg, which tracks.

## How to Befriend Crows

The most important thing is consistency. Crows are cautious, suspicious birds. They're not going to run up and eat out of your hand on day one. But if you show up at the same time, in the same spot, and reliably have food, they will notice you — and they will remember you specifically.

Crows recognize individual human faces. Not just "a person with food" — *you*. They can distinguish you from other people, and they pass that information to their family. This is part of why, once you've established a relationship with one crow, their partner and kids often start showing up too.

Start by leaving food on a ledge or the ground while you stand a comfortable distance away. Don't stare at them intensely or make sudden movements. Just be calm and present. Over time, they'll get closer. Eventually some of them will come specifically to find *you*, not just a food spot — I've had crows wait outside my building, follow me down the street, and land on my balcony railing.

It can take weeks or months, but some crows warm up faster than others. Merp figured out pretty quickly that I have peanuts and started actively seeking me out; Groof and Groofriend would land right in front of me and block my path if I tried to walk by without leaving treats. Every crow is different.

A few things that help:
- Be the one who gives food, not just someone who happens to be nearby when it's there
- Talk to them a little — they seem to pick up on voice along with face
- Don't bring other people over abruptly; let crows meet new people gradually
- If a crow is being shy, try crouching down or looking slightly away — direct eye contact can feel threatening

## What to Feed Them

**Peanuts in-shell** are my go-to. Crows love them, they're easy to carry, and they're safe. Crows will usually grab one or two, fly off to stash them, and come back. (They can caw with their beak full of peanuts, but honking requires their whole body, so the loud honkers tend to be empty-beaked.) Note that they can usually only carry one or two peanuts at a time — Green Bandit, a research crow from UW, figured out two but not three, and would call a friend over to help with the extra one.

**Eggs** are a favorite. I give hard-boiled eggs and they go nuts for them. One of my favorite things is watching them do "egg dippies" — carrying the egg over to a puddle and dipping it in the water, sometimes repeatedly. I've seen Fussy make absolutely spectacular messes eating eggs, just shredding the thing with total commitment.

**Dog or cat kibble** is another good option — nutritionally decent and easy to handle in bulk.

**Things to avoid:** salted or seasoned human food, bread (it fills them up with basically nothing), avocado, onion, chocolate. Basically: if it would be bad for a dog, assume it's bad for a crow.

A fun thing to try is putting treats inside a small bag or container. Some crows love the puzzle aspect. Merp learned a specific move — the "merp-dump" — where they grab the bag by the bottom and shake it until the treats fall out. I've also had crows tumble containers around, poke at them, and try multiple strategies. Pidgey (a specific pigeon who follows the crow gang around) could not figure out clear packaging at all, and eventually just started bullying the crows out of frustration.

**Squirrels** — worth mentioning here because they are absolutely a hazard at feeding spots. I learned this the hard way. They can move fast and get aggressive. Keep an eye out if there are squirrels nearby.

## Crow Trivia

Crows are genuinely one of the most intelligent animals on earth. Not "smart for a bird" — smart, full stop. They use tools, solve multi-step puzzles, and remember solutions across multiple sessions. Juvenile crows stay with their parents for a year or two to learn — and you can sometimes watch adults actively teaching younger ones.

I saw this happen with Groof's family: a younger crow was scared of an egg and kept walking away after pecking it, giving me this sad helpless look. Then an older crow came over and started chattering at them — this specific encouraging sound crows make — while demonstrating what to do. They have a whole range of calls: alarm caws, food calls, that encouraging chatter, contact calls between partners.

Some other things I find interesting:
- Crows hold "funerals" — large group gatherings around a dead crow. Researchers think they're learning about threats, like "whatever was near this dead crow is dangerous."
- They stash food constantly, and their spatial memory for stashes is impressive. Though I did see one crow completely lose their stash in a pile of leaves and spend a long time hopefully poking around trying to find it again. Relatable.
- Crow partners are very bonded. You'll often see a mated pair where one is pickier or bolder and the other hangs back a little. Longlegs and Fussy are like this — Longlegs stashes everything diligently, Fussy only eats their favorite things and makes a giant mess doing it.
- There's some speculation that crows might see in UV spectrum, which could be how they identify individual flock members. I think about this every time I see Groof looking kind of iridescent purple in certain lighting.

## Fledgling Info — Please Don't Pick Them Up

Every spring and early summer, people find young crows on the ground and assume they've been abandoned. They usually haven't been.

Fledglings are young crows who have left the nest but aren't great at flying yet. They hop around, look scraggly, make a raspy begging sound, and seem helpless. But their parents are almost always nearby — watching from a tree, coming down to feed them, teaching them what to do. The crow family knows where their kid is. The kid is supposed to be there.

If you pick up a fledgling and take it inside, you've separated it from its parents during a critical learning period. Please don't do this.

**What to do instead:**
- If it's in a genuinely dangerous spot (middle of a road, directly next to a cat), you can move it to a nearby shrub or low branch — within the same general area, just somewhere safer
- Watch from a distance to see if a parent comes. They usually will within an hour or two
- If the bird is visibly injured (not just grounded), or if you've watched for many hours with no parental visit, then contact a wildlife rehabilitator

You can tell a fledgling from an injured adult by the behavior: fledglings are excitable and hoppy, usually have a pink gape (the edge of their beak), and make that persistent raspy begging call. Adult crows in distress tend to act very differently.

## Misconceptions

**"Crows are attracted to shiny objects"**

This one is everywhere and it's mostly not true. Crows are actually pretty *neophobic* — nervous about new things, including shiny objects. They might investigate something shiny briefly, but they don't collect them. This myth also gets applied to magpies, and same deal there: studies haven't supported it. I think it comes from the general idea of crows as trickster/collector figures, which is vibes-based rather than biology-based.

**"If you touch a baby bird, the mother will reject it"**

False. Birds have a very limited sense of smell. A parent crow is not going to abandon their fledgling because a human touched it. This myth probably persists because people want a simple rule to keep kids from handling wildlife, which is a reasonable goal, but the actual reason is "let wildlife be wildlife and minimize stress to the animal," not scent contamination.

**"You shouldn't feed wild birds"**

This one's more nuanced. Supplemental feeding is generally fine as long as you're giving appropriate food and keeping the area reasonably clean (don't let old food accumulate and rot). The concern is usually about dependency, but crows are smart and opportunistic enough that you're supplementing their diet, not replacing it. The bigger risk is feeding in ways that attract huge flocks to inappropriate places, or feeding bad food. If you're giving a few crows peanuts on a walk, you're fine.

**"Crows are all the same / interchangeable"**

This one gets me. Once you spend any time with crows you realize how different they are from each other. Groof is big, round-beaked, bold, and instigates everything. Longlegs is methodical and a dedicated stasher. Fussy is picky and chaotic. Merp is clever and specifically learned to shake bags for treats. Green Bandit is unusually friendly and social, probably because of their UW research background. They are absolutely individuals with personalities and preferences.

**"Crows are bad omens / pests"**

Crows as bad omens is a long cultural tradition across a lot of different societies, which I find kind of charming, but it's vibes not data. As for pests: they're scavengers and opportunists, which means they'll get into trash and be annoying if you leave food accessible to them. But a crow who associates you with positive things is incredibly charming to have around. They're not trying to cause problems; they're just very smart and very hungry.
